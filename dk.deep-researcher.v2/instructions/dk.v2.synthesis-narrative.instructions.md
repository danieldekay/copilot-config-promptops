# Synthesis Narrative Writing

## Structure

### Opening

The narrative opens with two distinct sections:

**Abstract** (the "what"):

- Research question and scope
- Methodology overview (sources searched, quality tiers applied)
- Number and distribution of sources analyzed
- 100-150 words, factual and descriptive

**Executive Summary** (the "so what"):

- 3-5 key findings with confidence levels
- Most surprising or consequential finding
- Implications for the reader
- Recommended next steps
- 200-300 words, actionable and forward-looking

### Body (One Section Per Dimension)

1. Lead with the finding
2. Strongest evidence first (T1, then T2–3)
3. Note contradictions — present both sides
4. Quantify where possible
5. Cite inline — every factual claim

### Closing

- Cross-dimensional synthesis → implications → limitations → future directions

## Rules

1. **No source-by-source summaries** — synthesize across sources
2. **Evidence-first**: "The data shows X (Author, Year)" not "Author found X"
3. **Confidence qualifiers**:
   - Strong: "demonstrates", "confirms"
   - Moderate: "suggests", "indicates"
   - Weak: "preliminary evidence points to"
   - Contradicted: "evidence is mixed, with X supporting... and Y contradicting..."
4. **Active voice** — "Researchers found" not "It was found"
5. **Specific over vague** — "reduced latency by 40%" not "significantly improved"

## Cross-Reference Pattern

```markdown
This finding about {current state} connects to the {historical} challenge
identified by [Source]: the evolution from X to Y explains why the current
approach prioritizes Z ([Source 1]; [Source 2]).
```

## Gap Handling

When evidence is missing:

- State explicitly: "No sources address {dimension}"
- Hypothesize why: "This may be because..."
- Note as open question
- Do NOT fill gaps with speculation presented as evidence
